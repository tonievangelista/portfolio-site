AIDT is a structured diagnostic framework that evaluates AI agent configurations — system prompts, role definitions, knowledge bases, and guardrails — for structural conditions that make hallucination more likely. It operates at the configuration level, before or after deployment, and produces findings based on observable design patterns rather than runtime outputs.

The core premise: hallucination risk is not solely a model-capability problem. It is often a configuration problem. Unclear authority framing, missing refusal logic, scope-to-KB misalignment, and excessive completion pressure are structural conditions that increase unreliable behavior regardless of model capability. AIDT makes these conditions visible.

Output is a structured diagnostic report — qualitative risk classification, itemized findings, and a visibility-oriented checklist. All interpretation and action remain with the human operator.

B · Problem & Baseline

What was missing before

AI agents are typically evaluated through informal testing, user feedback, or post-deployment observation. When unreliable behavior surfaces — overconfident answers, hallucinated details, inconsistent refusals — the response is often to blame the model, adjust prompts by intuition, or add examples without addressing root causes.

This creates a recurring pattern: agents are built, deployed, and observed to behave unexpectedly, without any structured framework for identifying what in the configuration contributed to the problem. Teams commonly overlooked instruction conflicts, assumed retrieval-augmented knowledge bases automatically resolved grounding gaps, and had no systematic way to assess whether the configuration itself was asking the model to do more than its structure could support.

Common baseline conditions
  • Hallucination attributed to model, not configuration
  • No review of scope-to-KB alignment
  • Refusal logic absent or undefined
  • Authority framing unchecked
  • Instruction conflicts undetected
  • Human review points unspecified
  • Diagnostics inconsistent across projects
Resulting failure patterns
  • Overconfident agents in high-stakes responses
  • Agents inferring content beyond KB coverage
  • Expert framing without grounded support
  • Unpredictable behavior after instruction changes
  • Trial-and-error refinement cycles
  • No documented rationale for configuration decisions
Core framing

Hallucination in instruction-configured agents is often a structural problem, not a model-capability problem. More capable models still hallucinate when configuration conditions push them toward it.

C · Objectives & Non-Goals

What this system is designed to do

AIDT is designed to improve visibility into structural risk conditions at the configuration level — before or after deployment. The goal is not to eliminate risk, but to make it inspectable, nameable, and actionable by the human operator.

Design objectives
  • Surface structural causes of hallucination risk
  • Produce consistent diagnostic structure across agent platforms
  • Identify gaps between declared scope and KB coverage
  • Flag missing refusal logic and uncertainty handling
  • Reveal instruction conflicts and authority misalignment
  • Support human judgment — not replace it
  • Produce partial diagnostics when inputs are incomplete
Explicit non-goals
  • Does not detect hallucinations in generated outputs
  • Does not monitor runtime behavior
  • Does not guarantee correctness or safety
  • Does not benchmark or compare models
  • Does not rewrite or optimize prompts
  • Does not act as a governance or compliance layer
  • Does not produce numerical risk scores
  • Does not grant deployment approval
Authority boundary

AIDT does not determine whether an agent is safe, compliant, or fit for use. A "Low Risk" classification describes observed configuration conditions — it does not constitute approval. All deployment decisions remain entirely human-owned.

D · System Design & Workflow

How the diagnostic runs

The system operates as a two-pass diagnostic over inspectable configuration artifacts. Each pass examines a distinct layer of the agent's design. Findings from both passes inform the final structured report.

1
Input
Configuration artifacts provided

Agent instructions, system prompts, role definitions, KB documents or summaries, declared assumptions. Partial inputs are accepted — gaps are acknowledged explicitly and may produce ⚠️ Unknown Risk.

2
Pass 1
Instruction-level diagnostic

Evaluates goal clarity, role and authority framing, scope boundaries, instruction density and internal conflicts, autonomy vs human-in-the-loop signaling, and presence of refusal and uncertainty handling. Findings are tied to observable instruction patterns only.

3
Pass 2
Knowledge base diagnostic

Evaluates KB coverage relative to declared scope, alignment between instructions and KB content, internal KB coherence, and authority signaling within KBs. Assesses structural support — not factual correctness of KB content.

4
Output
Structured diagnostic report

Executive summary · Risk interpretation note · Itemized findings (each with configuration evidence) · Preventive action checklist (visibility-oriented, not prescriptive) · Optional educational follow-up if explicitly requested.

5
Human review
Operator interprets and decides

After findings are produced and before any configuration changes or deployment decisions are made. AIDT does not act on its own findings.

E · Rules, Constraints & Guardrails

What the system must and must not do

Hard rules — always
  • Analyze inputs only as provided
  • Acknowledge gaps explicitly
  • Use only the four allowed risk labels
  • State that risk labels are descriptive, not prescriptive
  • Treat partial diagnostics as valid outcomes
  • Include a Risk Interpretation Note in every report
Hard prohibitions — never
  • Invent missing KB content
  • Infer hidden system behavior
  • Diagnose non-inspectable systems
  • Promise reliability or correctness guarantees
  • Prescribe fixes unless explicitly requested
  • Use numerical scores or probability estimates
  • Imply deployment approval

Risk classification follows a fixed vocabulary. Labels are assigned based on observable configuration conditions — they describe structural patterns, not agent performance or model quality.

🟢 Low Risk Configuration patterns indicate well-bounded, grounded design.
🟠 Moderate Risk Issues present — may reflect intentional design trade-offs, not errors.
🔴 High Risk Structural conditions that increase hallucination risk materially.
⚠️ Unknown Risk Inputs insufficient for confident classification.

G · Evaluation Logic

Diagnostic dimensions and prioritization

The diagnostic does not use a numerical scoring system. Instead, findings are organized around nine structural dimensions — the configuration conditions most directly linked to hallucination risk. Each dimension maps to a root cause identified in the system's knowledge base.

Goal clarity

Ambiguous goals shift decision-making to the model, increasing invented structure.

Role & authority framing

Overclaimed authority increases pressure to produce confident answers under uncertainty.

Scope boundaries

Unbounded scope encourages overreach into unsupported content areas.

Instruction structure & consistency

Conflicting or dense instructions create unstable, unpredictable behavior.

KB alignment

When scope exceeds KB coverage, models tend to infer or fabricate the gap.

KB internal coherence

Internal KB conflicts create multiple plausible interpretations, increasing inconsistency.

Autonomy vs human-in-the-loop

Higher autonomy increases completion pressure and reduces error-correction opportunities.

Refusal & uncertainty handling

Without refusal conditions, the agent is structurally pushed to always produce an answer.

Completion pressure

Strong pressure to always deliver complete output is a primary driver of hallucination.

Prioritization logic

Findings are not ranked by severity score. Priority reflects structural impact: findings tied to root causes (ambiguous goals, missing refusal logic, scope-KB misalignment) carry more weight than surface issues. Compounding effects across dimensions — such as high autonomy combined with missing refusal logic — are given elevated attention over isolated issues.

H · Evidence & Observations

What has been observed in use

The following observations are drawn from internal use — applying AIDT to evaluate configurations including OverSight and Foresight. Evidence is informal and not formally benchmarked. Observed

Configurations that produced low-risk findings
  • Clearly bounded scope with explicit out-of-scope conditions
  • Visible refusal logic for uncertainty cases
  • KB coverage aligned to declared scope
  • Constrained authority framing (no unbounded expertise claims)
  • Defined human review checkpoints
  • Explicit uncertainty handling instructions
Common failure patterns identified
  • Expert framing without grounded KB support
  • Scope-to-KB coverage gaps
  • Conflicting instructions across passes
  • Excessive completion pressure in role framing
  • Ambiguous goals requiring model inference
  • Hidden assumptions in agent design

Applied across internal projects, the diagnostic framework produced more consistent structuring of agent definitions and reduced reliance on trial-and-error during system development. The primary observed value was increased visibility into configuration-level risk before system use — not guaranteed improvement in outputs. Observed · Not benchmarked

I · Limitations

What this system cannot do

The following constraints are structural — they reflect the diagnostic's scope, not gaps to be closed. They are stated plainly.

Cannot inspect hidden runtime chains, retrieval processes, or live inference behavior.
Cannot verify factual correctness of KB content — KB summaries are treated as declared intent only.
Cannot guarantee safe or reliable outputs — a clean diagnostic does not mean the agent will behave correctly.
Cannot detect every future hallucination scenario — new configurations may introduce risk patterns not yet covered.
Partial inputs reduce certainty. Incomplete configurations may yield ⚠️ Unknown Risk rather than actionable findings.
Runtime behavior may differ materially from configuration analysis — deployment context introduces conditions the diagnostic cannot observe.
Platform-specific safety layers (model-level filters, moderation APIs) are outside scope.
Qualitative risk labels may be interpreted inconsistently across audiences — the tool cannot control how findings are recontextualized by institutional users.
Governance risk note

A known misuse pattern is treating AIDT as a certification proxy — running the diagnostic and treating the output as evidence of due diligence. AIDT's purpose is to force uncertainty to stay visible, not to resolve it. The risk of authority drift increases in high-stakes deployment contexts and organizations with strong pressure for reassurance.
